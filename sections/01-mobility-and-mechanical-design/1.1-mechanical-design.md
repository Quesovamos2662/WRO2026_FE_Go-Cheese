# 1.1 Mechanical Components

This section covers the two active mechanical components responsible for every movement Cheese makes: the EV3 Large Motor and the EV3 Medium Motor. Unlike the chassis, which we rebuilt across three versions, our motor configuration stayed constant from the first digital model through the current competition build.

We selected LEGO motors over third-party units for one concrete reason: they connect directly to the EV3 brick and are controlled natively through our `ev3dev2` code, which eliminated the need for external motor drivers, custom wiring and calibration work. That decision cost us nothing in capability and returned weeks of development time we spent tuning behavior instead of debugging electronics.

---

## Motor Overview

| Component | Port | Function |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor** | `OUTPUT_B` | Rear-wheel drive. Supplies all propulsion through a direct 1:1 connection to the rear axle. |
| **EV3 Medium Motor** | `OUTPUT_A` | Ackermann steering. Controls the angle of the front wheels through the steering linkage. |

## 1.1.1 Drive Motor — EV3 Large Motor

**Component: LEGO EV3 Large Motor**

- **Quantity:** 1
- **Port:** `OUTPUT_B`
- **Top speed:** 170 RPM
- **Running torque:** 20 N·cm
- **Stall torque:** 40 N·cm
- **Operating voltage:** 9V
- **Weight:** 76 g
- **Function:** Drives the rear axle directly (1:1, no gear reduction). Carries the full 888.1 g mass of the robot forward and backward.

### Why we chose it

Propulsion on our robot is a torque problem before it is a speed problem. Every time Cheese starts from rest, the motor must overcome three forces at once: the inertia of a chassis approaching one kilogram, the rolling resistance of the tires against the mat, and the internal friction of the drivetrain. A motor optimized for RPM at the expense of torque would stall against that combined load, particularly at the moment of starting, where demand is highest.

The large motor's stall torque of **40 N·cm** is exactly double what the medium motor produces, and that is what breaks the robot cleanly out of a standstill and sustains it through resistance without bogging down mid-lap.

### How it contributes to performance

| Property | Benefit to the robot |
| :--- | :--- |
| **High rotational inertia** | The heavier rotor acts like a small flywheel, storing momentum and releasing it evenly. When the robot meets sudden drag (cornering resistance, surface inconsistencies), that stored momentum carries it through instead of letting the motor hesitate. |
| **Built-in encoder** | Because the drivetrain is 1:1, every degree of motor rotation equals one degree of wheel rotation. We convert encoder counts directly into distance traveled, giving the robot a progress measurement independent of the ultrasonic sensors. |
| **Smooth power delivery** | A drivetrain that surges and stalls produces a moving target no steering controller can tune against. Consistent delivery is what lets our PD controller work reliably. |

### Placement reasoning

The large motor's bulk fixed its position at the rear. Mounting it at the front would have placed it in direct competition for space with the steering assembly, which already occupies most of the front section, and would have concentrated a disproportionate share of the robot's mass directly over the steering axle, degrading exactly the part of the vehicle that most depends on being light and responsive.

### Drive Load Analysis

The EV3 Large Motor was selected because the drive system carries the highest mechanical load on Cheese. Unlike the steering motor, the drive motor is responsible for moving the entire robot mass across the mat. This means it must overcome resistance every time the robot starts, accelerates, corrects its path, or exits a curve.

| Load Type | How It Affects Cheese | Why the Large Motor Helps |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting inertia** | The robot resists movement when starting from rest. | Higher torque helps the robot begin moving without hesitation. |
| **Rolling resistance** | The tires and mat create resistance during motion. | The Large Motor keeps the rear wheels moving consistently. |
| **Drivetrain friction** | Axles, wheel connections, and LEGO parts create small losses. | Extra torque reduces the risk of slowing down under load. |
| **Correction load** | The robot still needs propulsion while steering corrections happen. | Smooth drive power keeps the robot moving while the steering system adjusts. |
| **Robot mass** | Cheese weighs close to 1 kg, so the motor must move a real load. | The Large Motor is better suited for moving the full chassis. |

This is why propulsion was treated as a torque problem first. Speed matters, but a fast motor would not help if it could not move the robot reliably under load. The Large Motor gave Cheese a stable drive foundation before we focused on steering and sensor tuning.

## 1.1.2 Torque and Speed Calculations

This section explains the torque and speed calculations used to justify Cheese’s drivetrain performance. The purpose of these calculations is to connect our motor selection, tire size, programmed speed values, drivetrain efficiency, and steering limitations to the robot’s real behavior on the track.

Cheese uses a direct **1:1 rear-wheel drivetrain**, meaning that one full rotation of the EV3 Large Motor produces one full rotation of the rear wheels. Because of this, the robot’s movement can be estimated from the motor RPM, the rear wheel circumference, and the programmed speed percentage used in the code.

---

### 1. Calculation Variables

| Variable | Value Used | Why It Matters |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor maximum speed** | 170 RPM | Maximum no-load speed of the drive motor. |
| **Normal programmed speed** | 62% | Speed used during regular PID-controlled driving. |
| **Gentle correction speed** | 46% | Reduced speed used during lighter wall corrections. |
| **Severe correction speed** | 40% | Reduced speed used during stronger wall corrections. |
| **Rear wheel diameter** | 5.6 cm / 0.056 m | Determines distance traveled per wheel rotation. |
| **Rear wheel radius** | 2.8 cm / 0.028 m | Used to estimate driving force from torque. |
| **Gear ratio** | 1:1 | Motor rotation and rear wheel rotation are equal. |
| **Large Motor running torque** | 20 N·cm | Torque used for normal drivetrain reasoning. |
| **Estimated drivetrain efficiency** | 85% | Accounts for mechanical losses from friction and drivetrain resistance. |
| **Robot mass** | ≈ 1 kg | Rounded value used for general mechanical reasoning. |

These values are important because Cheese does not always drive at full speed. The code uses different speed layers depending on the situation. During normal PID driving, the robot moves faster. During wall corrections, the robot slows down so the steering system has more time to react.

---

### 2. Normal Motor Speed During PID Driving

During normal driving, Cheese does not use the full **170 RPM** of the EV3 Large Motor. The programmed normal speed is approximately **62%**.

```text
RPM = RPMmax × programmed speed
RPM = 170 × 0.62
RPM = 105.4 RPM
```

| Value | Calculation | Result |
| :--- | :--- | :---: |
| **Maximum motor speed** | Given motor value | 170 RPM |
| **Programmed normal speed** | 62% = 0.62 | 0.62 |
| **Normal driving RPM** | 170 × 0.62 | 105.4 RPM |

This means that during normal PID-controlled movement, Cheese’s rear wheels rotate at approximately **105.4 RPM**. This speed is high enough to keep the run efficient, but lower than maximum speed so the PID controller still has time to correct the robot between the walls.

---

### 3. Rear Wheel Circumference

To convert wheel RPM into robot speed, we first calculate how far Cheese travels in one full rear wheel rotation.

The rear wheel diameter is **0.056 m**.

```text
C = π × d
C = π × 0.056 m
C ≈ 0.176 m
```

| Rear Wheel Measurement | Value |
| :--- | :---: |
| **Diameter** | 0.056 m |
| **Radius** | 0.028 m |
| **Circumference** | ≈ 0.176 m |

This means that every full rear wheel rotation moves Cheese forward by approximately **0.176 m**, assuming the tire does not slip.

---

### 4. Normal Driving Speed

Now that we know the normal motor RPM and the wheel circumference, we can estimate Cheese’s normal driving speed.

```text
v = (RPM × wheel circumference) ÷ 60
v = (105.4 × 0.176) ÷ 60
v ≈ 0.309 m/s
```

<p align="center">
  <strong>Cheese’s estimated normal driving speed at 62% power is approximately 0.31 m/s.</strong>
</p>

| Driving Mode | Programmed Speed | RPM | Estimated Speed |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Normal PID driving** | 62% | 105.4 RPM | ≈ 0.31 m/s |

This is the speed Cheese uses during regular wall-following movement. It is not the theoretical maximum speed of the drivetrain. Instead, it is the estimated speed produced by the actual programmed value used during normal driving.

In centimeters per second:

```text
0.309 m/s × 100 = 30.9 cm/s
```

<p align="center">
  <strong>At normal driving speed, Cheese travels approximately 30.9 cm per second.</strong>
</p>

This value matters because even small delays in steering can become important. If the robot travels about **30.9 cm every second**, then a late correction can move Cheese several centimeters closer to a wall before the steering finishes reacting.

---

### 5. Speed During Wall Corrections

Cheese uses lower speeds when it gets closer to the walls. This is a software decision connected to the mechanical limits of the steering system.

| Correction Type | Programmed Speed | RPM Calculation | Estimated RPM |
| :--- | :---: | :--- | :---: |
| **Gentle wall correction** | 46% | 170 × 0.46 | 78.2 RPM |
| **Severe wall correction** | 40% | 170 × 0.40 | 68 RPM |

The estimated speed for each correction mode is:

```text
Gentle correction:
v = (78.2 × 0.176) ÷ 60
v ≈ 0.229 m/s

Severe correction:
v = (68 × 0.176) ÷ 60
v ≈ 0.199 m/s
```

| Driving Mode | Programmed Speed | Estimated RPM | Estimated Speed |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Normal PID driving** | 62% | 105.4 RPM | ≈ 0.31 m/s |
| **Gentle wall correction** | 46% | 78.2 RPM | ≈ 0.229 m/s |
| **Severe wall correction** | 40% | 68 RPM | ≈ 0.199 m/s |

This shows that Cheese slows down as the correction becomes more serious. This matters because stronger wall corrections require more steering movement. If the robot stayed at normal speed while very close to a wall, it could crash before the steering angle finishes changing.

---

### 6. Torque Delivered Through the Drivetrain

The EV3 Large Motor has a running torque of **20 N·cm**. However, not all of this torque reaches the wheels perfectly. Some torque is lost through axle friction, drivetrain resistance, tire deformation, and small mechanical inefficiencies.

To account for this, we used an estimated drivetrain efficiency of **85%**.

```text
Torque at wheel = motor torque × gear ratio × drivetrain efficiency
Torque at wheel = 20 N·cm × 1 × 0.85
Torque at wheel = 17 N·cm
```

Then we convert from N·cm to N·m:

```text
17 N·cm = 0.17 N·m
```

| Torque Value | Result |
| :--- | :---: |
| **Large Motor running torque** | 20 N·cm |
| **Gear ratio** | 1:1 |
| **Estimated drivetrain efficiency** | 85% |
| **Estimated usable wheel torque** | 17 N·cm |
| **Estimated usable wheel torque** | 0.17 N·m |

This estimated value represents the usable torque that reaches the rear wheel during normal drivetrain operation after mechanical losses.

---

### 7. Estimated Driving Force at the Wheel

After estimating the usable torque at the rear wheel, we can estimate the driving force using the rear wheel radius.

```text
F = torque ÷ radius
F = 0.17 N·m ÷ 0.028 m
F ≈ 6.07 N
```

| Variable | Value |
| :--- | :---: |
| **Estimated usable torque** | 0.17 N·m |
| **Rear wheel radius** | 0.028 m |
| **Estimated driving force** | ≈ 6.07 N |

This force is an estimate of the horizontal driving force available at the rear wheel. It supports our decision to use the EV3 Large Motor for propulsion because the motor provides enough usable force to move Cheese’s mass without stalling during normal driving.

---

### 8. Speed and Torque Tradeoff

The drivetrain is a balance between **speed** and **control**.

The larger rear wheels help Cheese travel farther with each motor rotation, which increases speed. However, larger wheels also increase the radius, and a larger radius reduces the force produced at the ground for the same motor torque.

| Design Choice | Benefit | Tradeoff |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **56 mm rear wheels** | Higher distance per rotation and higher speed | Requires more torque than smaller wheels |
| **1:1 drivetrain** | Simple, direct, and easy to calculate | No gear reduction to multiply torque |
| **62% normal speed** | Efficient movement during straight sections | Steering has less reaction time than at lower speeds |
| **40–46% correction speeds** | Gives steering more time near walls | Reduces speed during correction zones |

We accepted this tradeoff because testing showed that the Large Motor can move Cheese reliably without stalling, and the wider rear tires provide enough traction for the drive system.

---

### 9. Why This Matters for Cheese

These calculations explain why Cheese uses different speed levels instead of driving at one constant speed.

| Situation | Speed Choice | Engineering Reason |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal straight driving** | 62% / ≈ 0.31 m/s | Fast enough for efficient laps while still allowing PID corrections. |
| **Gentle wall correction** | 46% / ≈ 0.229 m/s | Slows the robot slightly so steering can correct safely. |
| **Severe wall correction** | 40% / ≈ 0.199 m/s | Gives maximum correction time when the robot is close to a wall. |
| **Curves** | Controlled by curve logic | Steering needs time to reach the required angle. |
| **Parking** | Slower and more controlled | Final stop accuracy matters more than speed. |

The calculations show that speed cannot be chosen only by asking, “How fast can the robot go?” A better question is, “How fast can the robot go while still giving the steering and sensors enough time to react?”

## 1.1.3 Steering Motor — EV3 Medium Motor

**Component: LEGO EV3 Medium Motor**

- **Quantity:** 1
- **Port:** `OUTPUT_A`
- **Top speed:** 250 RPM
- **Running torque:** 8 N·cm
- **Stall torque:** 12 N·cm
- **Operating voltage:** 9V
- **Weight:** 36 g
- **Function:** Drives the Ackermann linkage, setting the angle of the front wheels. Contributes zero forward motion.

### Why the Medium Motor Was Chosen for Steering

Steering is a different mechanical task from propulsion. While the drive motor must provide continuous torque to move the entire robot forward, the steering motor must react quickly and repeatedly to small corrections. During a run, the front wheels constantly move from the center position toward one side, return through center, and correct toward the opposite side as the controller responds to sensor data.

For this reason, we selected the **EV3 Medium Motor** for the steering mechanism. Its lower rotational inertia makes it better suited for fast direction changes, allowing the steering system to start, stop, and reverse with less delay. A heavier motor would be more likely to introduce lag or overshoot during each correction, making the robot’s path less stable.

Because Cheese makes steering adjustments continuously while following the walls, even small delays can accumulate and create visible oscillation. The medium motor helps reduce that effect by making the steering response faster, lighter, and easier to control.


### How it contributes to performance

| Property | Benefit to the robot |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Low rotational inertia** | Reverses direction almost instantly, which is what continuous line correction demands. A heavy rotor would overshoot on every single correction. |
| **Built-in encoder** | Steering is about absolute position. The encoder lets us command an exact angle and hold it under braking, which is the mechanical foundation for proportional steering and reliable centering. |
| **Low mass (36 g)** | Less than half the large motor. Keeps the steering mechanism from sagging under the weight of its own actuator, which would introduce play into the linkage. |
| **Compact body** | The front is the most crowded region of the robot. This is the only EV3 motor that fits there without displacing the steering geometry. |

### Why the Medium Motor Fits the Steering Load

The EV3 Medium Motor does not need to move the full mass of the robot. Its job is limited to rotating the front steering linkage and changing the angle of the two front wheels. Because this load is much smaller than the load handled by the drive motor, high torque is not the main requirement for this subsystem.

For steering, response speed and precision matter more than raw pulling force. The medium motor provides enough torque to overcome the friction of the linkage while still reacting quickly to small corrections. This makes it well suited for our steering system, which is designed to prioritize fast, repeatable angle changes over brute force.


### Steering Response Requirement

The steering motor does not need to move the whole robot, but it must react constantly. During a run, Cheese makes many small steering changes while following the walls. These corrections happen repeatedly, so the steering motor must be able to move left, return to center, and move right without creating too much delay.

| Steering Requirement | Why It Matters |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Fast response** | The robot must correct its direction before reaching a wall. |
| **Repeatable movement** | The steering angle must behave consistently during PID corrections. |
| **Low mechanical load** | A lighter motor reduces stress on the front steering linkage. |
| **Accurate positioning** | The steering system must hold commanded angles during turns and corrections. |
| **Quick reversals** | The motor must change direction often without creating too much overshoot. |

This is why the Medium Motor is a better fit for steering than the Large Motor. The Large Motor has more torque, but its higher mass and heavier movement would make the steering system less responsive. For steering, speed and precision are more important than raw force.

### Steering Limitation Observed During Testing

Even though the Medium Motor is the correct choice for steering, testing showed that it still has a physical limit. When Cheese approaches a curve too quickly, the steering system may not finish reaching the commanded angle before the robot reaches the wall or corner.

This does not mean the Medium Motor is the wrong motor. It means the steering system needs enough time to complete its movement.

| Situation | What Happens | Design Response |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal straight movement** | Steering corrections are small and manageable. | Medium Motor works well for PID adjustments. |
| **Wall correction** | Steering angle may need to change more strongly. | The robot slows down to give steering more time. |
| **Fast curve entry** | The robot may reach the curve before steering finishes. | Curve speed and steering timing must be controlled. |
| **High linkage resistance** | Steering response becomes slower. | Linkage friction must be reduced mechanically. |

This observation helped us understand that steering performance depends on both mechanical design and software speed control. The motor can only steer correctly if the robot is moving at a speed that gives the linkage enough time to respond.

### Why Steering Is a Precision Task

Propulsion and steering are not equal jobs. The drive motor must provide force, while the steering motor must provide control. If the drive motor is slightly slower, the robot may simply lose time. However, if the steering motor reacts late, the robot can crash.

For that reason, the Medium Motor was selected because it gives Cheese a lighter and faster steering system. Its role is not to overpower the robot, but to make the robot’s direction changes more precise and repeatable.

## 1.1.4 Wheels and Tires

| Position | Tire | Diameter | Width |
| :--- | :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Front** | 43.2 x 22 ZR | 43.2 mm | 22 mm |
| **Rear** | 56 x 28 ZR | 56 mm | 28 mm |

Cheese runs mismatched tires by design: the rear tires are larger and wider than the front ones, and this has been true since our first build. The choice comes from the fact that the front and rear axles do completely different jobs.

**The rear tires set our speed:** Because the drivetrain is a direct 1:1 connection with no gear reduction, the rear tire diameter is the single mechanical variable that determines our top speed. Each rotation of the 56 mm rear tire moves the robot about 175.9 mm. A smaller tire would move less ground per revolution and cap our speed lower, so the larger rear tire is what gives us our theoretical ceiling of *roughly 0.5 m/s*.

**The rear tires carry all the torque:** The large motor delivers up to 40 N·cm of stall torque through the rear axle, and all of that force has to reach the mat through the rear contact patch. At 28 mm wide, the rear tires put noticeably more rubber on the surface than the 22 mm front tires, which reduces the chance of the drive wheels slipping when the motor delivers its full torque at startup.

**The front tires only steer:** The front wheels transmit no drive force at all; their only job is to point where the robot is going. That means they do not need width for traction or diameter for speed. A smaller, narrower tire is actually better here, because it is lighter, which reduces the load the medium motor has to swing back and forth on every steering correction, and it takes up less space in the crowded front section where the Ackermann linkage lives.

**The tradeoff we accepted:** A larger rear wheel gives the motor a longer lever to push against, which means the same torque produces less force at the contact patch than a smaller wheel would. We accepted this because the large motor has torque to spare at 40 N·cm, and because top speed mattered more to us than raw acceleration off the line.

## 1.1.5 nThe Two Motors as a Complementary System

The engineering value of this configuration comes from how the two motors divide the robot’s mechanical workload. Instead of expecting one motor to handle every task, we separated the system into two specialized functions: **propulsion** and **steering**.

The **Large Motor** handles the force-demanding task: moving the full mass of Cheese through the rear wheels. This requires higher torque, especially when the robot starts from rest or corrects its path while still moving forward.

The **Medium Motor** handles the precision-demanding task: rotating the front Ackermann steering linkage. This does not require moving the full robot mass, but it does require quick and repeatable angle changes as the control system reacts to sensor data.

Together, the motors form a balanced system: the Large Motor provides the force needed for movement, while the Medium Motor provides the faster response needed for steering accuracy.

| Alternative considered | Why we rejected it |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Two Large Motors** | Adds 40 g of unnecessary mass to the front. More damagingly, a heavy rotor cannot execute the rapid reversals continuous line correction demands. The robot would overshoot on every correction and oscillate down the straights. |
| **Two Medium Motors** | Excellent steering, but leaves the drivetrain with only 12 N·cm of stall torque to move an 888.1 g chassis. This invites stalling at every start and under the added load of cornering. |
| **Large + Medium (our choice)** | Each motor matched to the physical demands of its own task. Cheese drives with force and consistency at the rear while steering with speed and delicacy at the front. |

## 1.1.6 Testing and Observations

| System | What We Tested | Result | What It Taught Us |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rear tires** | Multiple speeds and repeated startups | No slipping observed | The rear tires provide enough traction for the drivetrain. |
| **EV3 Large Motor** | Full robot weight during movement | No stalling observed | The drive motor has enough torque for Cheese’s mass. |
| **EV3 Medium Motor** | Fast corner entries and steering corrections | Sometimes fails to reach the commanded angle in time | Steering response is the limiting factor during fast turns. |
| **Speed layers** | Normal, gentle correction, and severe correction speeds | Lower speeds improved control near walls | Software speed control helps compensate for steering limits. |

Choosing a motor from a specification sheet is useful, but testing the motor on the actual robot is what shows whether the choice works. Cheese’s drivetrain and steering system gave us two different lessons.

**The drivetrain has been reliable.** The EV3 Large Motor has been able to move the robot’s mass without stalling during normal testing. The rear wheels have also shown enough grip, since we did not observe slipping during repeated starts and speed changes. This supports our decision to use the Large Motor for propulsion and the wider rear tires for traction.

**The steering is the more sensitive system.** The EV3 Medium Motor is still the correct steering choice because it is lighter and faster than the Large Motor. However, during fast corner entries, the robot can sometimes reach the curve before the steering linkage finishes moving to the commanded angle. When that happens, Cheese may continue too straight and crash into the wall.

This means the main problem is not only mechanical and not only software. It is the interaction between both:

| Cause | Effect |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Higher driving speed** | Less time for steering to finish moving. |
| **Steering linkage resistance** | Slower steering response. |
| **Late curve reaction** | Robot enters the curve at a poor angle. |
| **Strong wall correction at high speed** | Robot may not recover before reaching the wall. |

Because of this, we use two improvement paths. Mechanically, we reduce resistance in the steering linkage and reinforce the structure so the steering system moves more freely. In software, we use speed layers so the robot slows down during correction-heavy situations.

<p align="center">
  <strong>Testing conclusion:</strong><br>
  The drivetrain proved reliable because the EV3 Large Motor and rear tires provide enough torque and traction for Cheese’s mass. The main limitation is not propulsion, but steering reaction time. Because of this, Cheese must manage speed through software layers so the steering system and sensors have enough time to react before walls, curves, and parking zones.
</p>

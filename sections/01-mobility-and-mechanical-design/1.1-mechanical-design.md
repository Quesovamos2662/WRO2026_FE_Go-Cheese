# 1.1 Mechanical Components

This section covers the two active mechanical components responsible for every movement Cheese makes: the EV3 Large Motor and the EV3 Medium Motor. Unlike the chassis, which we rebuilt across three versions, our motor configuration stayed constant from the first digital model through the current competition build.

We selected LEGO motors over third-party units for one concrete reason: they connect directly to the EV3 brick and are controlled natively through our `ev3dev2` code, which eliminated the need for external motor drivers, custom wiring and calibration work. That decision cost us nothing in capability and returned weeks of development time we spent tuning behavior instead of debugging electronics.

---

## Motor Overview

| Component | Port | Function |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor** | `OUTPUT_B` | Rear-wheel drive. Supplies all propulsion through a direct 1:1 connection to the rear axle. |
| **EV3 Medium Motor** | `OUTPUT_A` | Ackermann steering. Controls the angle of the front wheels through the steering linkage. |

---

## 1.1.1 Drive Motor — EV3 Large Motor

**Component: LEGO EV3 Large Motor**

- **Quantity:** 1
- **Port:** `OUTPUT_B`
- **Top speed:** 170 RPM
- **Running torque:** 20 N·cm
- **Stall torque:** 40 N·cm
- **Operating voltage:** 9V
- **Weight:** 76 g
- **Function:** Drives the rear axle directly (1:1, no gear reduction). Carries the full 888.1 g mass of the robot forward and backward.

### Why we chose it

Propulsion on our robot is a torque problem before it is a speed problem. Every time Cheese starts from rest, the motor must overcome three forces at once: the inertia of a chassis approaching one kilogram, the rolling resistance of the tires against the mat, and the internal friction of the drivetrain. A motor optimized for RPM at the expense of torque would stall against that combined load, particularly at the moment of starting, where demand is highest.

The large motor's stall torque of **40 N·cm** is exactly double what the medium motor produces, and that is what breaks the robot cleanly out of a standstill and sustains it through resistance without bogging down mid-lap.

### How it contributes to performance

| Property | Benefit to the robot |
| :--- | :--- |
| **High rotational inertia** | The heavier rotor acts like a small flywheel, storing momentum and releasing it evenly. When the robot meets sudden drag (cornering resistance, surface inconsistencies), that stored momentum carries it through instead of letting the motor hesitate. |
| **Built-in encoder** | Because the drivetrain is 1:1, every degree of motor rotation equals one degree of wheel rotation. We convert encoder counts directly into distance traveled, giving the robot a progress measurement independent of the ultrasonic sensors. |
| **Smooth power delivery** | A drivetrain that surges and stalls produces a moving target no steering controller can tune against. Consistent delivery is what lets our PD controller work reliably. |

### Placement reasoning

The large motor's bulk fixed its position at the rear. Mounting it at the front would have placed it in direct competition for space with the steering assembly, which already occupies most of the front section, and would have concentrated a disproportionate share of the robot's mass directly over the steering axle, degrading exactly the part of the vehicle that most depends on being light and responsive.

## 1.1.2 Steering Motor — EV3 Medium Motor

**Component: LEGO EV3 Medium Motor**

- **Quantity:** 1
- **Port:** `OUTPUT_A`
- **Top speed:** 250 RPM
- **Running torque:** 8 N·cm
- **Stall torque:** 12 N·cm
- **Operating voltage:** 9V
- **Weight:** 36 g
- **Function:** Drives the Ackermann linkage, setting the angle of the front wheels. Contributes zero forward motion.

### Why the Medium Motor Was Chosen for Steering

Steering is a different mechanical task from propulsion. While the drive motor must provide continuous torque to move the entire robot forward, the steering motor must react quickly and repeatedly to small corrections. During a run, the front wheels constantly move from the center position toward one side, return through center, and correct toward the opposite side as the controller responds to sensor data.

For this reason, we selected the **EV3 Medium Motor** for the steering mechanism. Its lower rotational inertia makes it better suited for fast direction changes, allowing the steering system to start, stop, and reverse with less delay. A heavier motor would be more likely to introduce lag or overshoot during each correction, making the robot’s path less stable.

Because Cheese makes steering adjustments continuously while following the walls, even small delays can accumulate and create visible oscillation. The medium motor helps reduce that effect by making the steering response faster, lighter, and easier to control.


### How it contributes to performance

| Property | Benefit to the robot |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Low rotational inertia** | Reverses direction almost instantly, which is what continuous line correction demands. A heavy rotor would overshoot on every single correction. |
| **Built-in encoder** | Steering is about absolute position. The encoder lets us command an exact angle and hold it under braking, which is the mechanical foundation for proportional steering and reliable centering. |
| **Low mass (36 g)** | Less than half the large motor. Keeps the steering mechanism from sagging under the weight of its own actuator, which would introduce play into the linkage. |
| **Compact body** | The front is the most crowded region of the robot. This is the only EV3 motor that fits there without displacing the steering geometry. |

### Why the Medium Motor Fits the Steering Load

The EV3 Medium Motor does not need to move the full mass of the robot. Its job is limited to rotating the front steering linkage and changing the angle of the two front wheels. Because this load is much smaller than the load handled by the drive motor, high torque is not the main requirement for this subsystem.

For steering, response speed and precision matter more than raw pulling force. The medium motor provides enough torque to overcome the friction of the linkage while still reacting quickly to small corrections. This makes it well suited for our steering system, which is designed to prioritize fast, repeatable angle changes over brute force.

## 1.1.3 Wheels and Tires

| Position | Tire | Diameter | Width |
| :--- | :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Front** | 43.2 x 22 ZR | 43.2 mm | 22 mm |
| **Rear** | 56 x 28 ZR | 56 mm | 28 mm |

Cheese runs mismatched tires by design: the rear tires are larger and wider than the front ones, and this has been true since our first build. The choice comes from the fact that the front and rear axles do completely different jobs.

**The rear tires set our speed:** Because the drivetrain is a direct 1:1 connection with no gear reduction, the rear tire diameter is the single mechanical variable that determines our top speed. Each rotation of the 56 mm rear tire moves the robot about 175.9 mm. A smaller tire would move less ground per revolution and cap our speed lower, so the larger rear tire is what gives us our theoretical ceiling of *roughly 0.5 m/s*.

**The rear tires carry all the torque:** The large motor delivers up to 40 N·cm of stall torque through the rear axle, and all of that force has to reach the mat through the rear contact patch. At 28 mm wide, the rear tires put noticeably more rubber on the surface than the 22 mm front tires, which reduces the chance of the drive wheels slipping when the motor delivers its full torque at startup.

**The front tires only steer:** The front wheels transmit no drive force at all; their only job is to point where the robot is going. That means they do not need width for traction or diameter for speed. A smaller, narrower tire is actually better here, because it is lighter, which reduces the load the medium motor has to swing back and forth on every steering correction, and it takes up less space in the crowded front section where the Ackermann linkage lives.

**The tradeoff we accepted:** A larger rear wheel gives the motor a longer lever to push against, which means the same torque produces less force at the contact patch than a smaller wheel would. We accepted this because the large motor has torque to spare at 40 N·cm, and because top speed mattered more to us than raw acceleration off the line.

---

## 1.1.4 The Two Motors as a Complementary System

The engineering value of this configuration comes from how the two motors divide the robot’s mechanical workload. Instead of expecting one motor to handle every task, we separated the system into two specialized functions: **propulsion** and **steering**.

The **Large Motor** handles the force-demanding task: moving the full mass of Cheese through the rear wheels. This requires higher torque, especially when the robot starts from rest or corrects its path while still moving forward.

The **Medium Motor** handles the precision-demanding task: rotating the front Ackermann steering linkage. This does not require moving the full robot mass, but it does require quick and repeatable angle changes as the control system reacts to sensor data.

Together, the motors form a balanced system: the Large Motor provides the force needed for movement, while the Medium Motor provides the faster response needed for steering accuracy.

| Alternative considered | Why we rejected it |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Two Large Motors** | Adds 40 g of unnecessary mass to the front. More damagingly, a heavy rotor cannot execute the rapid reversals continuous line correction demands. The robot would overshoot on every correction and oscillate down the straights. |
| **Two Medium Motors** | Excellent steering, but leaves the drivetrain with only 12 N·cm of stall torque to move an 888.1 g chassis. This invites stalling at every start and under the added load of cornering. |
| **Large + Medium (our choice)** | Each motor matched to the physical demands of its own task. Cheese drives with force and consistency at the rear while steering with speed and delicacy at the front. |

## 1.1.5 Torque and Speed Calculations

This section explains the main torque and speed reasoning behind Cheese’s drivetrain. Since our robot uses a direct **1:1 rear-wheel drive**, the rear wheel speed is directly connected to the EV3 Large Motor speed. This makes the drivetrain easier to calculate, easier to debug, and easier to explain.

The goal of these calculations is not to claim that Cheese always moves at this exact speed during a real run. Instead, the calculations give us a **theoretical mechanical ceiling**. The real speed will always be lower because the motor slows under load, the chassis has mass, and friction appears in the tires, axles, and drivetrain.

---

### 1. Drivetrain Configuration

Cheese uses a simple rear-wheel-drive setup. The EV3 Large Motor drives the rear axle directly, without gear reduction or gear multiplication.

| Drivetrain Feature | Value / Description | Engineering Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Drive motor** | EV3 Large Motor | Provides the torque needed to move the full robot. |
| **Drive location** | Rear axle | Rear wheels handle propulsion. |
| **Gear ratio** | 1:1 direct drive | One motor rotation equals one rear wheel rotation. |
| **Rear tire diameter** | 56 mm | Determines distance traveled per wheel rotation. |
| **Robot mass** | 888.1 g | Determines how much load the motor must move. |

The 1:1 drivetrain was chosen because it keeps the mechanical system simple. Since there is no gear reduction, the robot does not gain extra torque from gears, but it also avoids extra friction, backlash, and mechanical complexity. This makes the speed calculation more direct and makes the drivetrain easier to maintain.

---

### 2. Theoretical Speed Ceiling

The theoretical speed depends on two main values:

1. the free-running speed of the EV3 Large Motor  
2. the circumference of the rear tires  

The rear tire diameter is **56 mm**, so the circumference is calculated using:

```text
Circumference = π × diameter
Circumference = π × 56 mm
Circumference ≈ 175.9 mm
```

This means that every full rotation of the rear wheel moves Cheese forward by about **175.9 mm**, assuming no slipping.

| Variable | Value | Explanation |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Motor free-running speed** | 170 RPM | Maximum no-load motor speed. |
| **Rear tire diameter** | 56 mm | Diameter of the rear drive tire. |
| **Rear tire circumference** | 175.9 mm | Distance traveled per wheel rotation. |
| **Gear ratio** | 1:1 | Motor rotations and wheel rotations are equal. |

**Speed calculation:**

```text
170 rev/min × 175.9 mm/rev = 29,903 mm/min
29,903 mm/min = 29.903 m/min
29.903 m/min ÷ 60 = 0.498 m/s
```

<p align="center">
  <strong>Theoretical top speed: approximately 0.50 m/s</strong>
</p>

This number is the maximum theoretical speed under ideal conditions. In a real run, Cheese will move slower because the motor is not spinning freely. It is carrying the full weight of the robot, turning the drivetrain, overcoming rolling resistance, and sometimes slowing down for corrections, curves, and wall protection.

---

### 3. Torque Conversion at the Rear Wheels

Torque measures how strongly the motor can rotate the axle. For Cheese, this matters because the robot weighs **888.1 g**, so the motor must have enough rotational force to start the robot from rest and keep it moving during corrections.

The EV3 Large Motor provides:

| Torque Type | Value |
| :--- | :---: |
| **Running torque** | 20 N·cm |
| **Stall torque** | 40 N·cm |

To understand how this affects the ground, we can convert motor torque into an ideal wheel force. The rear wheel radius is half of the 56 mm diameter:

```text
Rear wheel radius = 56 mm ÷ 2 = 28 mm
Rear wheel radius = 0.028 m
```

Torque can be related to force at the wheel using:

```text
Force = Torque ÷ Radius
```

First, convert torque into N·m:

```text
20 N·cm = 0.20 N·m
40 N·cm = 0.40 N·m
```

Then calculate ideal force at the rear wheel:

```text
Running force = 0.20 N·m ÷ 0.028 m ≈ 7.14 N
Stall force = 0.40 N·m ÷ 0.028 m ≈ 14.29 N
```

| Torque Condition | Torque | Ideal Wheel Force |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Running torque** | 20 N·cm | ≈ 7.14 N |
| **Stall torque** | 40 N·cm | ≈ 14.29 N |

This is an ideal calculation before losses. In reality, the usable force is lower because of friction, tire deformation, drivetrain resistance, and motor load. However, it still shows that the EV3 Large Motor has enough force available to move Cheese’s mass reliably.

---

### 4. Robot Weight and Drive Load

Cheese weighs approximately **888.1 g**, or **0.8881 kg**. Its weight force can be estimated with:

```text
Weight = mass × gravity
Weight = 0.8881 kg × 9.81 m/s²
Weight ≈ 8.71 N
```

This does not mean the motor must constantly produce 8.71 N just to move forward. Weight acts downward, while the motor pushes horizontally through the tires. However, the weight matters because it increases normal force, rolling resistance, and the load the drivetrain must overcome.

| Quantity | Value | Meaning |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Robot mass** | 0.8881 kg | Total mass that must be moved. |
| **Estimated weight force** | ≈ 8.71 N | Downward force from gravity. |
| **Ideal running wheel force** | ≈ 7.14 N | Available drive force under running torque. |
| **Ideal stall wheel force** | ≈ 14.29 N | Maximum force before motor stalls. |

The drivetrain does not need to lift the robot; it needs to overcome rolling resistance and accelerate the robot forward. Testing showed that the EV3 Large Motor does this reliably, since the rear wheels did not slip and the motor did not stall during repeated runs.

---

### 5. Wheel Size Tradeoff

The rear tires are larger than the front tires by design. This is important because the front and rear wheels have different jobs.

| Wheel Position | Tire Size | Main Role |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Rear wheels** | 56 × 28 ZR | Drive, speed, and traction. |
| **Front wheels** | 43.2 × 22 ZR | Steering and direction control. |

The rear wheels are larger because they determine the robot’s distance traveled per motor rotation. A larger wheel increases top speed because each rotation covers more distance.

However, a larger wheel also creates a tradeoff: the same motor torque produces less force at the ground because the wheel radius is larger.

If Cheese used a smaller 43.2 mm tire as a drive wheel, the theoretical speed would be lower:

```text
Circumference = π × 43.2 mm ≈ 135.7 mm
170 rev/min × 135.7 mm/rev = 23,069 mm/min
23.069 m/min ÷ 60 ≈ 0.384 m/s
```

| Drive Tire Diameter | Circumference | Theoretical Speed |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **43.2 mm** | ≈ 135.7 mm | ≈ 0.384 m/s |
| **56 mm** | ≈ 175.9 mm | ≈ 0.498 m/s |

Using 56 mm rear tires gives Cheese a higher theoretical top speed. The tradeoff is that larger tires reduce the force delivered at the ground compared to smaller tires. We accepted this tradeoff because the EV3 Large Motor provides enough torque for the robot’s weight, and the wider 28 mm rear tires give enough traction to avoid slipping.

---

### 6. Why the Medium Motor Is Not Used for Drive

The EV3 Medium Motor is better for steering, not propulsion. It has less torque than the EV3 Large Motor, but it is lighter and faster. This makes it useful for moving the Ackermann steering linkage, where quick response matters more than raw pulling force.

| Motor | Best Use on Cheese | Reason |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor** | Rear-wheel drive | Higher torque for moving the full robot. |
| **EV3 Medium Motor** | Ackermann steering | Faster, lighter, and better for repeated angle changes. |

If the Medium Motor were used for drive, Cheese would have much less available torque to move the 888.1 g chassis. That would increase the risk of slow starts, poor acceleration, or stalling when the robot experiences extra resistance.

For this reason, the motor roles are separated:

- the **Large Motor** handles the force-demanding task,
- the **Medium Motor** handles the precision-demanding task.

This separation makes the drivetrain stronger and the steering system more responsive.

---

### 7. Speed vs. Control Tradeoff

Although a theoretical top speed of about **0.50 m/s** is useful, Cheese cannot always run at maximum speed. The robot must also react to walls, detect curves, avoid obstacles, and park correctly. If the robot moves too fast, the steering motor may not have enough time to reach the commanded angle before the robot reaches the wall or corner.

This means speed must be controlled by software. The robot can move faster in straight sections, but it may need to slow down before curves or during correction-heavy sections.

| Situation | Speed Requirement | Reason |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Straight movement** | Higher speed allowed | PID can make small corrections while the path is stable. |
| **Near walls** | Lower speed preferred | Gives wall protection more time to correct. |
| **Entering curves** | Controlled speed needed | Steering needs time to reach the turn angle. |
| **Obstacle detection** | Controlled speed needed | Robot needs time to react after HuskyLens detection. |
| **Parking** | Lower speed preferred | Improves final stop accuracy. |

The drivetrain is mechanically capable of moving fast, but the full robot must balance speed with steering response and sensor reaction time.

### 9. Engineering Summary

| Design Choice | Engineering Reason |
| :--- | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor for drive** | Provides enough torque to move the full robot reliably. |
| **EV3 Medium Motor for steering** | Provides faster response for repeated steering corrections. |
| **56 mm rear tires** | Increase distance traveled per wheel rotation and raise theoretical speed. |
| **28 mm rear tire width** | Provides more contact area for traction. |
| **43.2 mm front tires** | Reduce steering load and save space in the front mechanism. |
| **1:1 drivetrain** | Keeps the drive system simple, direct, and easy to calculate. |
| **Speed control in software** | Prevents the robot from outrunning its steering and sensors. |

The final drivetrain is a deliberate tradeoff between speed, torque, simplicity, and control. Cheese does not use gear reduction, so the system stays mechanically simple and reaches a higher theoretical speed. The cost is that the rear wheels demand more torque at the contact patch, but testing showed that the EV3 Large Motor can handle the robot’s weight without stalling or slipping.

  Cheese’s drivetrain is mechanically strong enough for the robot’s mass, while the software must control speed so the steering and sensors have enough time to react.
</p>

## 1.1.6 Testing and Observations

| System | What we tested | Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rear tires** | Multiple speeds, repeated startups | No slipping observed at any speed |
| **Large motor** | Full robot weight (888.1 g) | Never stalled, never struggled |
| **Medium motor** | Fast corner entries | Fails to reach the commanded angle in time → crashes |


Choosing a motor from a spec sheet is one thing. Watching it carry your robot around a track is another. We have run Cheese at many different speeds across many sessions, and those runs told us two very different stories.

**The drivetrain has never given us a problem.** Not once, at any speed we tried, have the rear wheels slipped. The large motor has never stalled or struggled under the weight of the robot, not even at startup, which is the hardest moment for any motor. This quietly confirmed the two claims our drivetrain design rested on: that 40 N·cm of stall torque is more than enough to move 888.1 g, and that the wider 28 mm rear tires deliver that torque without spinning out. We overbuilt this part of the robot, and we do not regret it, because it is the one system we never have to think about.

**The steering is where it hurts.** The medium motor cannot always turn the wheels fast enough. On a fast corner, the robot reaches the turn before its wheels have finished swinging to the angle the code asked for, and when that happens, Cheese drives straight into the wall. This is our single most frequent cause of crashing.

The frustrating part is that the medium motor is still the right choice. It is the fastest and lightest steering option the EV3 gives us. The problem is that we are asking it to complete a movement in less time than it physically has: as the robot's approach speed rises, the window to finish the steering swing shrinks, and eventually the motor runs out of time.

**What we take from this.** The crash is not a steering problem or a speed problem on its own, it is the collision between the two. That gives us two levers instead of one: slow the robot before the corner so the steering has time, or reduce the resistance in the linkage so the swing is faster. We pursued the second in our steering redesign, and the first is a software decision handled through our speed layers.

# ᯓ★ 1.2 Structural Components ᯓ★

This section covers the passive structural components that hold Cheese together: the Technic liftarms that form the frame, and the pins and axles that connect everything. These parts do not move the robot or sense anything, but they determine whether the robot holds its shape under load, and that turned out to be one of the hardest problems we faced.

## ❀ Technic Liftarms────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

**Why they work for us.** Liftarms are the backbone of our entire chassis. They are made of ABS plastic, which keeps them light while staying rigid, and their real strength comes from how they interact with Technic pins: when pinned together, liftarms form structures that resist bending in a way no single beam could. That rigidity is not cosmetic for us, it is functional, because our navigation runs on a PD controller that reads distance from the walls and corrects the steering. Every bit of flex or wobble in the frame introduces mechanical noise into those readings, and a controller cannot distinguish between the robot actually drifting toward a wall and the frame simply flexing. A rigid frame means the sensors report what is actually happening, which is the foundation the entire control system rests on.

Liftarms also enable cross-bracing, which is the technique of stacking them perpendicular to each other to form triangular structures. Triangles resist twisting in ways that squares do not, and this is what prevents the robot from torquing out of shape during sharp turns. Beyond structure, the liftarms' hole spacing follows an exact standard (1 stud = 8 mm), which allows us to position components at precise, repeatable locations. This is what made it possible to build the custom mount for our HuskyLens camera, since we could place it exactly where the camera needed to sit rather than approximately.

**Where they cost us.** Liftarms are not free. Each one weighs between 0.5 g and 4.5 g, and we use many of them, which contributes directly to our 888.1 g total mass. That mass is one of the factors limiting our top speed. There is also a skill gap we are honest about: using liftarms to their full potential requires knowing how to build efficient triangular structures that support weight while using as few parts as possible, and that is a technique we have not fully developed. Instead, we placed liftarms based on intuition and on what looked right, adding material where the structure felt weak rather than calculating where it actually needed reinforcement.

Their fixed 8 mm hole spacing also constrains us geometrically. Ackermann steering depends on precise linkage lengths and pivot positions, and when every hole must fall on an 8 mm grid, we cannot always place a pivot exactly where the geometry would ideally want it. We work within that grid rather than around it.

**What this means for the robot.** Liftarms are simultaneously the reason our chassis holds together and the main constraint on its performance. They gave us a rigid, rebuildable frame, and they let us iterate quickly when a design failed. But our intuition-driven approach to placing them means we are carrying weight we may not need. At 888.1 g, the robot's weight is approximately 8.71 N of force (0.8881 kg × 9.81 m/s²) pressing down through the drivetrain, and reducing that mass through smarter liftarm placement is one of the clearest paths we have to improving performance.

## ❀ Technic Pins and Axles────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

**Why they work for us.** Pins and axles are what turn a pile of liftarms into a robot. Pins connect liftarms, motors, and sensors into a single structure, and their friction fit is what lets us choose whether a joint stays locked in place or rotates freely, which is essential in a steering linkage where some joints must pivot and others must not move at all. They also allow rapid modification: a pin can be pulled and reinserted in seconds, which meant that during testing we could adjust the structure between runs rather than rebuilding it.

Axles serve a different purpose. They transmit rotation, and specifically they transmit the torque from the large motor to the rear wheels, which use a 3L axle with stop. Unlike pins, axles do not bear the load of holding pieces together, so they do not wear out from friction over time. Axles also come in standard lengths, and with 3D printing we have the option of producing custom lengths when a standard one does not fit.

**Where they cost us.** Technic pins are the weakest link in our build, and we mean that literally. They are prone to breaking when they bear weight without additional support, and they can shatter under handling. Worse, they wear down gradually: the friction that makes them grip is the same friction that degrades them, so a pin that starts tight becomes loose over dozens of runs. Once grip weakens, the ordinary vibration of the robot driving is enough to work pieces apart. Axles have their own failure mode: they require bushings to hold components like wheels in place, and if a bushing is not properly tightened, the component shifts and the geometry misaligns, which is exactly what we cannot afford in a steering system that depends on precise angles.

**How this shaped our redesign.** This fragility is the direct reason our physical build required significantly more pins and connectors than our BrickLink model predicted. Because a single pin holds only a small load before it starts to slip, reinforcing a weak joint meant surrounding it with several pins rather than trusting one. The digital model assumed ideal connections that hold whatever we asked of them; the real build did not, and every joint that failed during testing had to be rebuilt with redundant pinning. This is the same underlying problem described in the Chassis Explanation section, where the gap between the virtual model and the physical structure drove most of our redesign.

**What we would change.** Despite all of this, we would not replace pins and axles. Their greatest weakness, the fact that they pull apart under force, is also their greatest strength during development, because it is what let us modify the chassis in minutes instead of hours. Our one real complaint is the material: pins would benefit enormously from a harder plastic that resists bending and does not degrade with repeated use.

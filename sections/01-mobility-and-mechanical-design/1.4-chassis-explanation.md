# ᯓ★ 1.4 Chassis Explanation ᯓ★

Cheese is not built around a single frame. It is built around the EV3 brick, with the steering and drive assemblies attached to a structure that holds the brick up and ties everything together. This section explains how that structure is put together, and how it changed as we learned what it actually needed to do.

| Specification | Value (v3) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Length** | ~28 cm |
| **Width** | ~13 cm |
| **Height** | ~27 cm |
| **Weight** | 888.1 g |
| **WRO size limit** | 30 x 20 x 30 cm |

---

## ❀ 1.4.1 How the Chassis Is Assembled ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

**The EV3 sits on top of the structure, not inside it.** The brick is raised on a supporting structure rather than resting directly on the frame. This lifts it clear of the drivetrain and gives us room underneath to route the linkage and mount components.

**The front and rear are joined by pin connectors.** The steering assembly at the front and the motor assembly at the rear are connected using pin connectors joined with Technic pins. This is the link that makes the robot one piece rather than two halves.

**The sides balance the brick against the wheels.** On both lateral sides of the EV3 there are structural parts that connect out to the wheels. These keep the brick and the chassis in balance with each other, and the Technic pins are what let us tighten that whole system down so it holds together as one unit.

## ❀ 1.4.2 Building With What We Had ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

Not every part on Cheese is genuine LEGO. Some of our pieces are third-party, and that was not a design decision, it was a resource one.

With the number of teams working from the same material pool and a shortage of parts to go around, we could not always get the LEGO piece the build called for. So we sourced what we could find, and used whatever would do the job of holding the structure together. It worked, but it comes with a cost we should be honest about: third-party parts do not always match LEGO's tolerances exactly, and that mismatch is part of why some of our connections were looser than they should have been.

---

## ❀ 1.4.3 Where the Design Came From ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

Almost every problem in this chassis traces back to one root cause: **we designed v1 entirely in BrickLink Studio before building it with real parts.**

When we recreated that digital model with the actual kits, most of the modeled structures turned out to have serious problems with their connections and stability. The software assumes ideal parts that fit together perfectly. Our real build mixes genuine LEGO with third-party pieces, and the tolerances do not always match. Every joint that held on screen did not necessarily hold in our hands.

The consequence was that the physical robot needed far more pins and connectors than the model called for. That is not a criticism of BrickLink; it is a lesson about what a digital model can and cannot tell you.

---

## ❀ 1.4.4 What We Changed, and What We Changed Back ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

**The EV3 orientation.** In v1 the brick stood vertically. From v2 onward it lies flat, horizontally, with its buttons and display facing up toward the large motor at the rear. In the original layout the start button faced the front of the robot, which made it awkward to reach when we needed to launch a run. Rotating the brick opened up space between it and the large motor, and now our hands reach the buttons easily.

**Lengthening the chassis, and then undoing it.** We lengthened the front of the chassis to gain stability, accepting a slower, heavier car in exchange for a steadier one. Then we reversed the decision and shortened it back, because a shorter chassis gives us better correction on the steering angle, and by that point we had improved the assembly enough that the extra length was no longer buying us what we thought it would.

This is worth stating plainly, because it is the kind of thing that is tempting to leave out: we made a change, tested it, and undid it. The lesson was that the instability we were trying to fix with length was actually coming from loose fit and weak connections, not from the geometry. Once we tightened the chassis, the length stopped mattering.

**Tightening rather than rebuilding.** The reinforcement in v3 was not about adding structure for its own sake. There were parts of the infrastructure near the medium motor that felt loose, and that looseness fed straight into the steering, which is the last place a robot can afford play. We tightened those connections, and they are solid now.

---

## ❀ 1.4.5 What Tightening the Chassis Actually Did ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

The clearest result of the v3 work was not something we measured with a scale or a ruler. It was something we noticed in how the robot behaved.

Once we tightened the connections, Cheese started listening to its code. The robot became more precise, and it followed the instructions we gave it more faithfully than it had before. The same commands that used to produce approximate movements now produced the movements we actually asked for.

That is the whole argument for a tight chassis in one observation. Every loose joint sits between the code and the wheels, absorbing part of the instruction before it ever reaches the ground. A command to steer 25 degrees becomes 22 degrees by the time it passes through play in the linkage and flex in the frame. We were not fighting our code; we were fighting the slack in our own structure.

---

## ❀ 1.4.6 Weight Across Versions ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

| Version | Weight | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **v1** | Not measurable | Digital model only. BrickLink assumes ideal LEGO parts, and our real build mixes LEGO with third-party pieces, so the model's estimate would not have been accurate. |
| **v2** | 763.5 g | First physical build |
| **v3** | 888.1 g | Current build |

The robot gained about 125 g between v2 and v3, and it is worth being clear about where that weight came from. It was **not** the chassis. It was the HuskyLens camera and the custom mount we built to hold it.

That is a trade we took without hesitation. The camera is what makes the Obstacle Challenge possible at all, and 125 g is a small price for an entire round of the competition.

---

## ❀ 1.4.7 Chassis Iterations ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

| Version | Chassis state | What drove the change |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **v1** | Compact, EV3 vertical, digital model only | Built for speed on the assumption that smaller is faster |
| **v2** | EV3 horizontal, first physical build | Real parts exposed the connection problems the model hid |
| **v3** | Shortened back, tightened, reinforced near the medium motor | Fixing the fit solved what we had tried to solve with length |

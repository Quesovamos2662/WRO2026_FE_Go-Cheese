# ᯓ★ 2.1 Power Supply & EV3 ᯓ★

Everything on Cheese runs off a single source: the EV3's rechargeable battery. There is no secondary supply, no external pack, no separate feed for the camera. Every motor, every sensor, and the entire vision system draws from the same brick. This section explains how that power is distributed, why voltage matters more than we expected, and what fails first when it runs out.

---

## ❀ 2.1.1 The Power Source ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

| Item | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Battery** | LEGO EV3 rechargeable Li-ion battery pack |
| **Capacity** | 2050 mAh |
| **Nominal voltage** | ~7.4V (rises above nominal when fully charged, falls as it drains) |
| **Number in rotation** | 2 |
| **Powers** | EV3 brick, both motors, 2 ultrasonic sensors, color sensor, Arduino Nano, HuskyLens |

We run two battery packs in rotation. While one is on the robot, the other is charging, and we swap them when the first runs down.

This is not about convenience. Battery voltage measurably changes how Cheese behaves, which means **a run on a half-drained battery is not testing the same robot as a run on a full one.** Rotating packs is what lets us tune against a consistent machine instead of a moving target.

The EV3 exposes its battery voltage to our code, so we can read the exact level at any point during a run rather than guessing from behavior.

---

## ❀ 2.1.2 How Power Is Distributed ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

Cheese has a centralized power architecture. The EV3 rechargeable battery acts as the main power source for the robot, while the Arduino Nano receives 5V through the EV3 USB port and then powers the HuskyLens camera through its 5V rail.

| Component | Port | Powered by | Approx. Voltage |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **EV3 Large Motor** | `OUTPUT_B` | EV3 battery, direct motor output | ~7.4V nominal through EV3 motor driver |
| **EV3 Medium Motor** | `OUTPUT_A` | EV3 battery, direct motor output | ~7.4V nominal through EV3 motor driver |
| **Ultrasonic sensor (left)** | `INPUT_1` | EV3 battery, through sensor port | ~5V logic/sensor supply |
| **Color sensor** | `INPUT_2` | EV3 battery, through sensor port | ~5V logic/sensor supply |
| **Ultrasonic sensor (right)** | `INPUT_3` | EV3 battery, through sensor port | ~5V logic/sensor supply |
| **Arduino Nano** | `USB` | EV3 battery, through USB rail | 5V |
| **HuskyLens camera** | I2C through Arduino Nano | Arduino Nano 5V and GND pins | 5V |
| Helping lamps | 2 | Lighting support | Powered by external 9V battery |

The last two rows are the ones that matter most. **The Nano draws from the EV3's USB rail, and the HuskyLens draws from the Nano.** The camera is two steps removed from the battery, which means it is the most vulnerable component on the robot to a voltage drop, and as we explain below, it is the first thing to die.

The lighting system uses two auxiliary lamps powered by a separate 9V battery. The upper lamp helps illuminate the obstacle detection area for the HuskyLens camera, while the lower lamp improves floor visibility for the color sensor.

## ❀ 2.1.3 Why Voltage, Not Charge, Is What Matters ────୨ৎ────────୨ৎ────

A battery does not hold a constant voltage as it discharges. A Li-ion pack sits above its nominal voltage when fully charged and falls steadily as it drains. That falling voltage is what actually drives our robot's behavior, and here is why it matters mechanically.

**In a DC motor, speed is proportional to voltage.** The relationship is:

`no-load speed ∝ V_supply`

So when the supply voltage falls by 10%, the free-running speed of both motors falls by roughly the same 10%. The motors do not fail gracefully at some cutoff; they slow down continuously, in proportion to how much voltage is left.

**Torque, meanwhile, is proportional to current.** A drained battery has higher internal resistance, so under the same load it delivers less current, which means less torque. The large motor has enough headroom (40 N·cm stall) that this rarely becomes a problem for propulsion.

The medium motor is where it hurts, and that is the subject of the next section.

> **To be measured:** we will log our battery voltage at the start of a fresh pack and at the point where the robot begins failing, and record the percentage drop between them.
